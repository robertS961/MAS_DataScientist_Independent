Data Science & Citation Analysis

A multi-faceted exploration of abstracts, citations, downloads, and temporal dynamics in a conference corpus.

Introduction

This report presents an integrated exploratory and explanatory analysis of a scholarly conference dataset. We apply unsupervised text modeling to abstracts to discover latent research topics, construct a baseline predictive model for citation counts, compare citation distributions across paper types, analyze download behavior, and examine temporal citation trends. Each section provides visual evidence, an interpretation of findings, and a discussion of implications for researchers, editors, and data scientists. Collectively, these analyses illustrate how textual, categorical, and temporal signals combine to furnish a multifaceted picture of research impact.

Introduction to Topic Modeling and Findings: Topic modeling using LDA provides a compact, interpretable projection of recurring themes across the corpus of paper abstracts. The horizontal bar charts display the top ten lexical indicators (words) contributing to each latent topic; the relative bar lengths indicate how strongly each token contributes to the topic definition. Interpreting these topics permits researchers and program committees to summarize large corpora, identify emergent subfields, and inform downstream analyses such as citation modeling and thematic trend detection. In the next section we leverage bibliometric variables to quantify how content signals relate to measurable impact.
Predictive Modeling of Citation Counts: We fit a simple linear regression to estimate CrossRef citation counts from two readily-available predictors: publication year and Aminer citation counts. The scatter plot compares predicted versus actual citation counts for test-set observations, with a dashed 1:1 reference line indicating perfect prediction. While this baseline model has limited explanatory power (see the R² and RMSE in the chart title), it provides a transparent starting point for understanding citation dynamics and highlights the need for richer covariates (e.g., topic, venue prestige, author networks). This analysis motivates the categorical comparison that follows.
Effect of Paper Type on Citation Distribution: The box plots show how citation counts are distributed across paper types, emphasizing medians, interquartile ranges, and outliers. Differences in central tendency or spread suggest that certain formats (e.g., full papers, short papers, demos) systematically attract different levels of scholarly attention. Researchers can use this evidence to set expectations for impact by submission type, while program organizers may consider these differences in evaluation and track design. The variation observed here also encourages inquiry into download behavior, which we analyze next, to see whether readership precedes or predicts citation accumulation.
Exploratory Analysis of Download Patterns: The histogram depicts the empirical distribution of Xplore download counts, restricted to the 95th percentile to provide a clear picture of the central bulk of observations and to avoid visual distortion from extreme values. The skewed distribution and presence of heavy tails indicate that a small fraction of papers receive disproportionately high attention. Such asymmetry is common in scholarly metrics and suggests further study into what drives visibility (for example, topical interest, open access, or author prominence). Linking downloads to citation outcomes is a natural next step and complements the temporal trends we present subsequently.
Temporal Trends in Citation Activity: The time-series plot presents year-by-year averages of CrossRef citation counts, with interactive controls (range slider and selection buttons) to facilitate focused inspection of short- and long-term trends. This overview illuminates macro-level changes—such as rising or declining average citation counts—that may reflect shifts in community practices, dataset composition, or the maturation of particular research themes. Temporal analysis is essential for forecasting and for designing temporal stratification in predictive models; detailed causal interpretation requires controlling for confounders such as cohort size and topical shifts.

Conclusion

In summary, topic modeling surfaces coherent lexical themes that can structure downstream analyses; a simple regression baseline demonstrates the partial predictability of citation counts from common bibliometrics but also underscores the limits of naive models. Paper type and download distributions reveal important heterogeneity in scholarly attention, while temporal trends contextualize impact across cohorts. Future work should integrate topic signals, network features, and richer author/venue covariates into predictive pipelines, and perform rigorous causal or experimental studies to discover mechanisms that drive scholarly impact.

Reproducibility note: Visualizations were generated with Plotly and a simple scikit-learn pipeline. Numeric missing values were imputed with column medians; abstracts were analyzed using LDA after standard token filtering. For production analyses, consider documenting preprocessing steps, versioning data, and conducting sensitivity analyses.
