Exploratory Analysis of Conference Publications: Citation Dynamics, Dissemination, and Recognition

This report presents a structured, analytic exploration of a corpus of conference publications. We synthesize temporal citation patterns, model the association between paper attributes and formal recognition (awards), investigate drivers of dissemination (measured by downloads), and map topical evolution through keyword trends. Our approach balances descriptive visual analytics with predictive modeling to yield both interpretable summaries and testable hypotheses about what characterizes high-impact or recognized work. The documentation below follows a logical progression: from global trends, to classification and regression modeling, to topic dynamics and inferential comparisons — facilitating a coherent narrative for researchers and program committees alike.

1. Temporal Patterns in Citations

We begin with a longitudinal examination of mean citation counts as recorded by two complementary sources (CrossRef and Aminer). The line plots summarize central tendency across publication years and indicate how scholarly attention changes over time; in particular, they reveal whether the corpus exhibits secular growth in citations, episodic spikes corresponding to emergent topics, or attrition in attention for older venues. At the conference level, small-multiple time-series enable a comparative assessment of venue-specific trajectories: some conferences may sustain consistently higher mean citations while others display volatility driven by a small number of influential papers. These visual diagnostics are essential for identifying temporal confounders prior to any downstream inferential or predictive modeling.

2. Predicting Award Recognition (Classification)

We operationalize award prediction as a binary classification problem using a Random Forest classifier. Features include temporal indicators (year), dissemination metrics (downloads), topical density (keyword counts), reference structure (internal reference counts), and citation-derived signals. Categorical covariates for conference and paper type are reduced to dominant levels to retain interpretability and to control for venue effects. The ROC curve provides a concise summary of discrimination performance (AUC). Importantly, feature importances from the ensemble model highlight which predictors are most informative in this observational setting; these results should be interpreted as indicators of association rather than causal determinants. Together, these outcomes can guide further qualitative investigation into the criteria by which committees select award winners.

The following section presents distributional comparisons and inferential tests that probe whether awards are associated with higher citations or downloads.

5. Comparative Analysis: Awards, Citations, and Downloads

This section contrasts the empirical distributions of citations and downloads for awarded versus non-awarded papers. Violin plots present distributional shape and dispersion concurrently with embedded boxplots to summarize central tendency and spread. We apply nonparametric Mann–Whitney U tests as a robust inferential check for median differences when sample sizes permit. In the present dataset, the Mann–Whitney test yields a citation p-value of 5.853996120320115e-18 and a downloads p-value of 3.120121099829474e-35, indicating some statistical evidence of difference (subject to sample size and multiple-testing caveats). Interpretation of these comparisons should account for selection mechanisms and potential confounders; awards may be driven by intrinsic novelty, community visibility, or committee-specific criteria that are not fully captured by citation or download counts.

Conclusion and Future Directions

In this report we integrated descriptive time-series visualizations, supervised learning, regression diagnostics, topical trend analysis, and inferential comparisons to characterize a corpus of conference publications. The analyses provide evidence that citation and download dynamics are multifactorial — influenced by temporal context, venue identity, topical prominence, and other observable attributes — and that predictive models can surface correlates of recognition and dissemination. For future research, we recommend more granular causal or longitudinal modeling (e.g., difference-in-differences, survival analyses of citation accrual), richer text-derived features from abstracts, and cross-validation with external datasets to enhance generalizability. Ultimately, combining quantitative modeling with qualitative program-committee insight will yield the most actionable interpretations for shaping conference and publication policy.
