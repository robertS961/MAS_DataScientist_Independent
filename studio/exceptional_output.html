Interactive Data Science & Statistical Learning Report

This report presents a multi-faceted exploration of a curated conference publications dataset using descriptive time-series analyses, predictive machine learning, regression modeling, survival analysis, and comparative impact assessment. The primary objectives are to characterize temporal citation dynamics across paper types, evaluate predictors associated with award-winning papers, model the determinants of document downloads, examine the lifecycle of paper popularity, and quantify the relationship between awards and impact metrics. Each section includes interactive visualizations, an interpretive academic narrative, and guidance for future investigation. The sections follow a logical progression from descriptive trends to inferential and predictive modeling, facilitating a coherent understanding of observed patterns and their implications.

Figure 1 — Trend Analysis

The first analysis inspects mean citation trajectories over time for the five most prevalent paper types. By visualizing both Aminer-derived citations and CrossRef citations (toggleable via the control in the plot), we obtain a comparative perspective on citation accrual as recorded by different sources. This approach is important because it reveals longitudinal differences in scholarly attention that may arise from disciplinary conventions, paper typology (e.g., full paper vs. short paper), or community adoption dynamics. In practice, divergent trends between Aminer and CrossRef values may point to heterogeneous indexing coverage or to variations in downstream citation behavior; observing consistent increases or declines within particular paper types can inform authors and program committees about long-term visibility and influence. The subsequent section builds on these descriptive insights by modeling which features predict award outcomes, thereby linking temporal impact to characteristics associated with exceptional recognition.

Figure 2 — Predictive Modeling (Awards)

This section presents a logistic regression model evaluated with a Receiver Operating Characteristic curve to estimate the ability to discriminate award-winning papers from the remainder. Features incorporated in the preprocessing pipeline include year, downloads, categorical indicators such as paper type and conference, and textual TF-IDF encodings of author keywords and internal references. The AUC summarizes discriminative power: a high AUC would indicate that the chosen features reliably separate award recipients from others, whereas a modest AUC suggests additional latent factors (e.g., novelty, reviewer preferences, or committee-specific criteria) govern awards. Understanding these limitations points to opportunities for enhanced feature engineering — such as semantic topic embeddings or network centrality measures — to increase predictive fidelity. The following regression analysis leverages the same set of predictors to quantitatively model download counts and understand continuous impact measures.

Figure 3 — Regression for Downloads

Here, a multivariable linear regression predicts Xplore downloads using citation measures, counts of internal references, publication year, and conference affiliation. The scatter plot contrasts observed versus predicted downloads, with the dashed identity line indicating perfect prediction; summary statistics (R² and RMSE) in the title quantify explanatory power and average error. This analysis is valuable for isolating which measurable features correlate with readership, guiding authors and conference organizers about attributes that may increase dissemination. Notably, heteroskedasticity, omitted variables (e.g., promotion, author prominence), and nonlinearity can limit the linear model; these limitations motivate the temporal survival analysis that follows, which reframes popularity as a time-to-event process rather than a static endpoint.

Figure 4 — Survival Analysis of Popularity

The survival analysis models the time until a paper attains at least the median number of downloads, stratified by major paper type groups. Kaplan–Meier curves provide an intuitive probability that a paper remains below this download threshold as it ages; steeper drops indicate faster attainment of median popularity. Interpreting these curves allows stakeholders to compare the typical lifecycle of different paper types and to infer whether certain formats enjoy faster uptake. From a methodological standpoint, this approach accommodates right-censoring and reframes popularity dynamics in temporal terms, suggesting future extensions to Cox proportional hazards regression or time-varying covariates to formally test the influence of conference, year, and award status on the hazard of reaching popularity.

Figure 5 — Award Impact Comparison

The final visualization compares distributions of CrossRef citations and Xplore downloads for award-winning versus non-award papers using faceted box plots. This comparative analysis probes the empirical association between formal recognition and subsequent measures of impact. While median differences or interquartile spread may suggest a relationship, these descriptive results should be interpreted cautiously due to selection effects and potential confounders (for instance, topically salient papers or those from prominent authors are both more likely to win awards and to attract citations). The figure motivates formal inferential testing, propensity-matching, or longitudinal causal models to better establish whether awards drive impact or primarily signal pre-existing advantages.

In conclusion, the set of analyses synthesizes descriptive and predictive approaches to characterize scholarly visibility in conference publications. Trends across paper types highlight heterogeneous citation trajectories; predictive modeling identifies the limits of readily available features to anticipate awards; regression modeling and survival analysis frame downloads as both an outcome and a temporal process; and comparative plots elucidate the empirical relationship between awards and impact metrics. Collectively, these results emphasize the value of richer feature sets (e.g., semantic embeddings, author networks, and temporal promotion signals) and more rigorous causal designs in future work. The interactive figures provided herein serve as a reproducible foundation for deeper inquiry into the determinants of scholarly influence.
