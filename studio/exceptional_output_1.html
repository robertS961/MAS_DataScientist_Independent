Introduction: This report examines a curated conference dataset to elucidate temporal citation dynamics, the predictive relationship between bibliometric and contextual features and award attainment, factors that predict download activity, topical keyword evolution, and the empirical association between awards and scholarly attention. We combine descriptive aggregation, supervised learning, and simple natural language processing to create an integrated analytical narrative. The subsequent sections present interactive visualizations that support reproducible inspection and enable domain experts to draw operational and theoretical inferences.

1. Trend Analysis: Citation Trajectories

Trend Analysis (Figure 1): The first visual analyzes average citation trajectories across years for the most frequent paper types, comparing two citation sources (Aminer and CrossRef). By aggregating at the year level and faceting by citation source, we illuminate heterogeneities in how different classes of contributions accrue citations over time, and highlight potential divergence between citation indices. This comparison is important because index-specific coverage and lag effects can bias assessments of scholarly impact; practitioners should therefore consider multiple citation sources when benchmarking publications. The temporal patterns identified here motivate modeling approaches that follow, where citation and contextual signals are used to predict awards and downloads.

2. Predicting Awards: Model Evaluation and Coefficients

Award Prediction (Figure 2): The second visualization presents the performance of a logistic regression model trained to predict the binary award flag and the magnitude of model coefficients. The left panel reports the ROC curve and associated AUC, providing a threshold-independent assessment of discriminative capacity. The right panel shows coefficient estimates for features such as year, conference encoding, paper type encoding, download counts, and citation measures, which together indicate relative predictive importance and sign (positive or negative). Interpreting these coefficients in concert with their practical magnitudes can reveal which signals are most associated with award recognition; however, one must account for confounding and the coarse encoding of categorical variables. The insights drawn here naturally lead to a focused regression analysis of downloads, which may capture a downstream measure of public interest linked to awards.

3. Download Prediction: Linear Regression Coefficients

Download Regression (Figure 3): This section examines a linear regression that models downloaded counts as a function of temporal and bibliometric predictors including year and citation measures as well as encoded categorical signals. Coefficient magnitudes reflect the marginal association between each predictor and downloads, holding other variables constant; positive coefficients suggest features that increase expected downloads while negative ones suggest a relative suppression. These results help stakeholders prioritize dissemination strategies and pinpoint whether bibliometric visibility or conference affiliation correlates more strongly with readership. The analysis also motivates the exploration of topical drivers, which we address in the keyword trend section below.

4. Keyword Trend Analysis: Top Topics Over Time

Keyword Trends (Figure 4): Using author-provided keywords, we identify the five most frequent topical labels and track their annual frequencies. This lightweight natural language processing approach reveals emergent or waning research themes and permits stakeholders to observe shifts in community attention over time. Understanding such topical trajectories is key for strategic program planning, reviewer selection, and anticipating future directions in the field. Observed topic dynamics further contextualize why certain papers garner awards or higher downloads, thereby linking content to impact metrics.

5. Awards and Scholarly Attention

Awards and Citations (Figure 5): The box plot comparison contrasts CrossRef citation distributions between awarded and non-awarded papers. This descriptive comparison highlights central tendency and dispersion differences; if awarded papers exhibit systematically higher medians or reduced variance, it may indicate that awards are selective for high-impact work or alternatively that awards amplify attention. Such interpretations must be tempered by selection bias and temporal effects (e.g., older awarded papers have more time to accumulate citations). The subsequent download comparison complements this view by showing whether award-related differences extend to readership metrics.

Awards and Downloads (Figure 6): Mirroring the citation comparison, this visualization compares download distributions for awarded versus non-awarded papers. Downloads capture a different dimension of engagementâ€”often upstream of citationsâ€”and can be responsive to outreach and visibility. Noting differences between the two box plots (citations vs downloads) provides a more nuanced understanding of the mechanisms of scholarly attention. Collectively, these descriptive results frame the concluding section where we synthesize implications and recommend avenues for further analysis.

Conclusion

Conclusion: The integrated exploratory and modeling analyses presented in this report reveal that citation trajectories, conference and paper-type signals, downloads, and topical focus interact in complex ways around scholarly recognition. Logistic models provide a pragmatic baseline for award prediction, while regression coefficients for downloads identify features linked to readership. Keyword trends supply interpretive context that connects content to impact. Future work could extend these analyses using more sophisticated text embeddings, temporal survival models for popularity decay, and causal inference approaches to better isolate the effect of awards from confounders. Finally, analysts should account for index-specific coverage and reporting lags when operationalizing these findings for decisions.
